Algorithmic Generation of Constrained Circular Lithophanes via Browser-Based Three.jsIntroduction to Constrained Topographical Additive ManufacturingThe digital synthesis of lithophanic artifacts represents a highly specialized intersection between computational optics, computational geometry, and the material physics of additive manufacturing. Historically originating as hand-carved porcelain artworks in the nineteenth century, lithophanes operate on the fundamental principles of optical attenuation, most accurately described by the Lambert-Beer law. This law dictates that the transmission of light through a translucent medium decreases exponentially as a function of the material's thickness. In the contemporary context of Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) and Fused Filament Fabrication (FFF), this optical phenomenon is harnessed by utilizing computer algorithms to convert two-dimensional digital grayscale imagery into a three-dimensional topographical map. By carefully modulating the Z-axis elevation of a translucent thermoplastic polymer, such as Polylactic Acid (PLA) or Polyethylene Terephthalate Glycol (PETG), an FDM printer can create a physical surface that, when illuminated from behind, recreates the original image with striking photographic fidelity.While the broader principles of lithophane generation are well documented, this analysis is governed by an exceedingly rigid and highly constrained operational paradigm. The objective is to programmatically generate a precisely dimensioned circular lithophane with a maximum diameter of exactly 35 millimeters. The vertical topology is strictly discretized into four specific layer heights, each measuring 0.3 millimeters, resulting in a maximum overall thickness of 1.2 millimeters and an allowable quantization of 0.3mm, 0.6mm, 0.9mm, and 1.2mm. Furthermore, the geometric synthesis must account for the mechanical limitations of a standard 0.4-millimeter extrusion nozzle.Crucially, this entire generative pipeline must be executable exclusively on the client-side within a standard web browser utilizing JavaScript and the WebGL-wrapper library Three.js. Because modern web development increasingly relies on automated coding assistants, the underlying mathematical logic and application programming interface (API) calls must be structured in a modular, highly deterministic manner that is highly amenable to generation via Large Language Models (LLMs). This constraint requires an architectural departure from legacy Three.js practices, necessitating the implementation of explicit data array manipulations and mathematically robust error-handling mechanisms that LLMs can reproduce without succumbing to API hallucinations or outdated documentation patterns.The Optics and Mechanics of the 0.4mm NozzleTo construct an algorithm capable of producing a physically viable lithophane, the software must be explicitly informed by the electromechanical and fluid dynamics of the 3D printer's extrusion apparatus. The most consequential hardware parameter in FDM manufacturing is the diameter of the extrusion nozzle, which in this specified architecture is 0.4 millimeters.Defining the Minimum Printable Feature SizeThe 0.4-millimeter nozzle establishes an absolute lower boundary on the spatial resolution achievable in the XY plane, a metric formally defined as the Minimum Printable Feature Size (MPFS). When a slicer program processes a standard geometric mesh, it translates the digital vertices into physical machine instructions (G-code) that dictate the travel paths of the print head. If the algorithmic generator feeds the slicer a digital mesh containing topographical details, sharp gradients, or isolated peaks that measure less than 0.4 millimeters across, the slicing engine faces an unresolvable mechanical conflict. The slicer will typically respond in one of two ways: it will either mathematically average and completely erase the sub-nozzle detail, or it will attempt to extrude microscopic volumes of plastic, causing the extrusion motor to rapidly retract and advance. This latter scenario results in mechanical hysteresis, internal pressure fluctuations within the nozzle melt zone, and physical defects known as "stringing" or "oozing," which permanently ruin the optical clarity of the lithophane.The MPFS constraint demands a direct mathematical translation of the 35-millimeter circular boundary into a discrete pixel grid. Given the 35mm diameter and the 0.4mm nozzle, the absolute maximum number of parallel toolpaths (lines of extruded plastic) that can cross the lithophane is determinable through basic division.$$\text{Maximum Feature Resolution} = \frac{\text{Diameter of Lithophane}}{\text{Diameter of Extrusion Nozzle}} = \frac{35\text{ mm}}{0.4\text{ mm}} = 87.5\text{ Features}$$This calculation reveals a profound algorithmic limitation: regardless of whether the user supplies a high-definition 4K resolution image or an 8-megapixel photograph, the physical 35mm lithophane can only render a maximum equivalent resolution of approximately $87 \times 87$ physical "pixels". Providing the three-dimensional rendering engine with a mesh that has a vertex density higher than this physical limit is computationally wasteful and mathematically detrimental to the print outcome. Therefore, the JavaScript algorithm must scale and discretize the source image mapping to match this physical grid prior to any topographical displacement.JavaScript Canvas Pre-Processing and Spatial FilteringIn a browser-based workflow, image data ingestion is universally handled via the HTML5 Canvas API. The source image is drawn onto an off-screen canvas using the drawImage() method, and the raw RGBA (Red, Green, Blue, Alpha) pixel array is extracted via the getImageData() method, returning a one-dimensional Uint8ClampedArray. Because the raw image invariably contains high-frequency details that exceed the 0.4-millimeter MPFS limit, spatial filtering must be applied across this pixel array to attenuate noise and prevent the generation of unprintable micro-geometry.Convolution Kernels and Gaussian BlurTo ensure that spatial transitions in the lithophane are smooth enough for continuous nozzle travel, the algorithm must implement a low-pass spatial filter. In JavaScript, this is accomplished by traversing the Uint8ClampedArray and applying a mathematical convolution kernel. The Gaussian blur is the most efficient and practical filter for browser-based JavaScript execution, operating by recalculating each pixel's luminance based on a weighted average of its surrounding neighbors.The Gaussian distribution function applied to the two-dimensional pixel grid dictates that the central pixel receives the highest mathematical weight, while the influence of neighboring pixels decreases exponentially relative to their distance from the center. For a lithophane scaled to the $87 \times 87$ physical resolution limit, a $3 \times 3$ or $5 \times 5$ Gaussian kernel provides the optimal balance between noise reduction and detail preservation. While more advanced algorithms like Non-Local Means (NLM) or median filtering exhibit superior edge-preservation characteristics in theoretical imaging, they carry a high algorithmic time complexity ($O(n^2)$ per neighborhood search) that can freeze the main thread of the browser environment. However, mapping a continuous photographic image to a 2-bit (four-level) depth using simple thresholding results in catastrophic posterization. Gradients are destroyed, transitioning abruptly from one elevation to the next, which leaves visually harsh contour lines across the lithophane's surface and fails to utilize the optical blending required to simulate continuous tone. To recover the visual perception of continuous depth while strictly adhering to the four physical layer heights, the algorithm must employ sophisticated dithering techniques.Error Diffusion versus Ordered DitheringDithering algorithms trick the human eye into perceiving continuous gradients by varying the spatial distribution of quantized values. The most famous of these algorithms is the Floyd-Steinberg error diffusion algorithm. In a JavaScript implementation, Floyd-Steinberg scans the image array from left to right, top to bottom. When it forces a pixel into one of the four discrete layer heights, it calculates the "quantization error"—the mathematical difference between the original continuous value and the new discrete value. It then pushes fractions of this error forward to neighboring, unvisited pixels according to a precise distribution matrix (7/16 to the right, 3/16 to the bottom-left, 5/16 directly below, and 1/16 to the bottom-right).While Floyd-Steinberg produces beautifully smooth photographic results on two-dimensional screens, it is mechanically catastrophic for three-dimensional additive manufacturing with a 0.4mm nozzle. Error diffusion operates by scattering isolated pixels in chaotic, randomized patterns. If mapped directly to 3D geometry, an isolated pixel translates into a microscopic pillar or a microscopic hole measuring roughly 0.4mm by 0.4mm. To print this, the 3D printer must halt its primary path, travel to the isolated coordinate, extrude a micro-droplet of plastic, retract the filament, and travel away. Multiplying this behavior by tens of thousands of scattered pixels results in print times extending into days, rampant stringing, nozzle jamming, and ultimate mechanical failure of the artifact.To circumvent this mechanical incompatibility, the generation algorithm must abandon error diffusion in favor of Ordered Dithering via a Bayer Matrix. Unlike error diffusion, ordered dithering compares the continuous grayscale value of each pixel against a predefined threshold map (the Bayer matrix) that is tiled repeatedly across the image coordinates.A standard $4 \times 4$ Bayer matrix contains values from 0 to 15, distributed to maximize the distance between sequential numbers:$$M = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 8 & 2 & 10 \\ 12 & 4 & 14 & 6 \\ 3 & 11 & 1 & 9 \\ 15 & 7 & 13 & 5 \end{bmatrix}$$When implemented in the JavaScript Canvas logic, the position of the pixel modulo the size of the matrix determines the specific threshold. Because the Bayer matrix enforces a rigid, cross-hatched mathematical structure rather than random noise, the resulting quantized pixels cluster into highly predictable, continuous geometric bands.
